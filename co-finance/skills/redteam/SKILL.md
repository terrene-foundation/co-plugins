## What This Phase Does

Challenge your work the way a tough but fair professor would — testing argument strength, evidence quality, methodology soundness, and source credibility. Academic rigor testing, not proofreading.

## Your Role (Student)

Review the feedback and decide how to respond. Not every critique requires a change — sometimes acknowledging a limitation is the right response. But every critique deserves consideration.

## Workflow

### 1. Read all drafts

Review everything: all sections, data analysis, charts, citations, supporting materials. Understand full scope before critiquing.

### 2. Challenge each major claim

For every significant argument: Is the evidence sufficient? Are counterarguments addressed? Does the conclusion follow from evidence? Is the claim appropriately scoped (not overclaiming or underclaiming)?

### 3. Check methodology

For empirical/quantitative work: Is methodology appropriate? Assumptions stated and justified? Limitations acknowledged? Data reliable and cited? Calculations correct? Results interpreted carefully (correlation vs. causation, statistical vs. practical significance)?

### 4. Verify citations and sources

Are sources credible (peer-reviewed, established)? Attribution correct? Sources current enough? Sufficient breadth (not over-relying on one source)? Citation format consistent?

### 5. Report findings

Present as constructive academic feedback, organized by severity:
- **Major concerns** — significantly weaken the work; must address before submission
- **Moderate suggestions** — would meaningfully improve the work
- **Minor refinements** — polish items to elevate quality
- **Strengths** — what the student has done well (always include)

For each finding: what the issue is, why it matters, and how to address it.

### 6. Iterate until convergence

If the student revises: re-read, check whether revisions address concerns, identify new issues, continue until the work is as strong as it can be.
